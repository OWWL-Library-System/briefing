## Directors' Q&A - January 14, 2026

## Banking deposits and insurance limits

**Question:** We receive our town check once a year. Our balance sits above deposit insurance coverage for roughly three to five months. What should we do?

**Key fact check:** FDIC coverage is generally $250,000 per depositor, per insured bank, per ownership category. Coverage can vary based on how accounts are titled and structured, so "checking plus CDs at the same bank" is not automatically separate coverage.

**Main risks to consider:**

- Bank failure exposure for funds above insured limits.
- Audit and governance risk if the board cannot show it understood and managed cash concentration risk.

**Actionable options:**

- Ask your bank to explain, in writing, how your library's deposits are insured based on your specific account titling and ownership categories (for board files and audit trail).
- Reduce peak balances at one institution by spreading funds across multiple FDIC-insured banks (example: move reserves or the annual lump deposit portion to a second bank).
- Ask the bank about municipal or collateralized deposit options if available (you want clarity on whether and how amounts above FDIC limits are secured).

## Budgeting for vacation and sick time

**Question:** How do we put vacation and sick time into the budget?

**Main risks to consider:**

- Underbudgeting coverage costs (overtime, substitutes) when leave usage spikes.
- Inconsistent practice year to year, which makes year-end budget amendments more likely.

**Actionable options:**

- Treat paid leave as part of normal payroll and benefits planning for each position.
- Add a separate coverage line for predictable backfill costs (substitutes, overtime), using prior-year actuals to set a defensible estimate.
- Track leave-driven overtime and temporary coverage costs during the year so you can adjust next year's budget assumptions.

## Front desk customer service, mission alignment, and technology support

**Question:** How do we train new and existing front desk staff on customer service, the library's mission, and basic tech troubleshooting?

**Main risks to consider:**

- Inconsistent service delivery and avoidable patron escalations.
- Staff doing "off-mission" tech support that creates liability or unrealistic community expectations.

**Actionable options:**

- Define "always" behaviors in a short checklist (example: greet promptly, clear closing language, consistent privacy practice).
- Practice short role-play scenarios in staff meetings (policy disputes, upset patrons, boundary-setting, and escalation paths).
- Make escalation rules explicit (what staff handle, what goes to a supervisor, what is a safety issue).
- For technology: set a clear service boundary. Staff must know library platforms (catalog, e-content, databases) and where to refer patrons for non-library tech needs.

## Generative AI in policies and collection development

**Question:** Should we add policy language about staff and board Gen AI use, and should we amend the Collection Development Policy?

**Main risks to consider:**

- An overly rigid "ban" that becomes unenforceable once AI-assisted creation becomes common in publishing workflows.
- Low-quality or unreliable materials entering the collection, especially via self-published pathways.

**Actionable options:**

- For staff and board: adopt an internal "appropriate use" approach (define permitted uses, privacy and confidentiality limits, and review expectations).
- For collection development: avoid a bright-line "no AI" rule. Instead, strengthen your quality and selection criteria so low-quality materials fail on quality, accuracy, and suitability grounds.
- For local author donations: ensure your Gifts and Donations Policy clearly states that donations are evaluated under the Collection Development Policy and are not automatically added.

### Example Policy Clause

> The Library selects materials based on quality, accuracy, relevance, and suitability for the community, consistent with this policy.
>
> The Library recognizes that some content may be created or assisted through automated or generative technologies. The presence or absence of such technologies, by itself, does not determine whether material is selected or excluded.
>
> Selection decisions are based on the following principles:
>
> - Materials are evaluated using the same professional review standards applied to all formats and sources.
> - Staff prioritize authoritative, accurate, well-reviewed, and appropriate content for the Library's service population.
> - Materials that demonstrate poor quality, factual inaccuracies, lack of editorial oversight, or misleading content may be excluded, regardless of how they were produced.
> - The Library does not automatically add self-published or donated materials, including those created with generative tools. All such materials are reviewed under this policy before inclusion.
> - The Library reserves the right to decline materials that do not meet collection standards, even if they are locally produced or donated.
>
> This approach allows the Library to:
>
> - Maintain collection quality and integrity.
> - Address emerging publishing practices without restricting future formats or tools.
> - Defend selection decisions based on policy criteria rather than production method.

## Treasurer oversight, internal controls, and role clarity

**Question:** What should a treasurer do, and what internal controls should we prioritize?

**Main risks to consider:**

- Weak segregation of duties (one person initiates, records, reconciles, and approves).
- Missing or incomplete monthly reporting, which limits board oversight.

**Actionable options:**

- Ensure the board receives regular treasurer reporting that covers all funds, not only operating cash.
- Separate key duties where feasible (payment initiation, check preparation, signing/authorization, and reconciliation review).
- Require independent review of bank reconciliations (example: prepared by one party, reviewed by a different party, with documented sign-off).

## Treasurer appointment and oath of office

**Question:** Does the treasurer need an oath of office, and when?

**Key fact check:** New York Public Officers Law requires a public officer to take and file the oath of office before performing official duties.

**Main risks to consider:**

- Compliance risk if the oath is not filed before duties are performed.

**Actionable options:**

- Build oath collection and filing into your annual reappointment workflow for officers.
- The Treasurer position is typically an annual appropriation requiring an oath to be filed for each year.

## Board minutes, agendas, and attachments retention

**Question:** How do retention requirements differ for minutes versus agendas and meeting attachments?

**Key fact check:** LGS-1 requires official minutes (and records accepted as part of the minutes) to be retained permanently.

**Key fact check:** LGS-1 allows meeting files not accepted as part of the minutes, including agendas and background materials, to be retained for 1 year.

**Main risks to consider:**

- Destroying records too early, or inconsistently applying your retention rule (creates FOIL and credibility risk).

**Actionable options:**

- Decide what you treat as "part of the minutes" versus "meeting files," then apply LGS-1 consistently.
- If you keep records longer than the minimum, apply the longer retention consistently across the same record type to avoid cherry-picking risk.
- Create a Records Retention Policy that is at a minimum compliant with the LGS-1.

## Posting board packets online prior to meetings

**Question:** What must be posted online 24 hours before a public meeting?

**Key fact check:** New York's Open Meetings Law guidance indicates that, at least 24 hours prior to a meeting, documents scheduled to be discussed that board members have received should be posted online for the public to see.

**Main risks to consider:**

- Open Meetings Law compliance risk if materials are not posted when required.

**Actionable options:**

- Use a repeatable workflow: publish the agenda and board packet in one consistent location at least 24 hours before the meeting.
- Keep an internal archive of the exact packet posted for each meeting (for records management and audit support).
- Materials may most likely be removed after the meeting minutes are posted to the website.

## Memorials, gifts, and donations record retention

**Question:** How long do we keep records related to memorials and donations?

**Key fact check:** LGS-1 item 45 requires permanent retention when a trust fund or endowment is involved, and permanent retention for gifts of art or historical artifacts. Other gifts or bequests not covered in those categories are 6 years.

**Main risks to consider:**

- Treating a restricted or endowment-style gift like an unrestricted donation, leading to compliance and donor-intent risk.

**Actionable options:**

- Classify the gift correctly at intake: endowment or trust, artifact or artwork, or standard gift. Apply the matching LGS-1 retention period.
- Ensure your Gifts and Donations Policy clearly states acceptance terms, including whether gifts may be transferred or disposed of when no longer useful.

## Evergreen pending patrons and online library card registration

**Question:** How should we categorize self-registered applicants in Evergreen, and what do we do with out-of-system online applicants?

**Main risks to consider:**

- Inconsistent patron categorization, which affects reporting, permissions, and staff workflow.
- Identity verification gaps if online applicants are treated as fully registered before in-person confirmation.

**Actionable options:**

- Follow the OWWL Evergreen policy for Online Library Card Registration and Pending Patrons, including the recommended patron profiles and conversion steps after identity verification.
  - <https://docs.owwl.org/Evergreen/PatronsPendingPatrons>
  - <https://docs.owwl.org/Evergreen/OwwlPolicy#Online_Library_Card_Registration_Policy>
- Treat applicants as "online" status until they complete your library's verification process and are converted to the standard patron profile.

## Staff attendance and last-minute call-ins

**Question:** How can libraries consistently and equitably address frequent last-minute call-ins?

**Main risks to consider:**

- Uneven enforcement that creates morale issues, grievances, or discrimination claims.
- Service disruption and unplanned overtime costs.

**Actionable options:**

- Set clear expectations in writing for call-in procedures, including notice requirements where feasible, with an explicit carve-out for truly sudden illness.
- Watch for patterns. When a pattern exists, use progressive documentation and intervention consistent with your personnel rules and any civil service requirements.
- Document behavior and impact using observable facts (dates, times, call-in timing, coverage impacts), not interpretations.

## Workplace conduct and addressing passive-aggressive behavior

**Question:** When a staff member repeatedly undermines new employees through passive-aggressive behavior, when is a performance improvement plan appropriate?

**Main risks to consider:**

- Allowing a hostile or disruptive work environment to persist, increasing turnover and service quality problems.
- Weak documentation that cannot support corrective action.

**Actionable options:**

- Document specific observable behaviors (what was said or done, when, who was present, and the work impact).
- Tie expectations to conduct standards (professional communication, responsiveness to work requests, and respectful collaboration).
- Use a time-bound improvement plan with clear expectations and check-ins, then follow your progressive discipline process if behavior does not change.

## Municipal relationships, authority, and control of library funds

**Question:** When a municipality holds library funds, what authority does the town have over library money and decisions?

**Key fact check:** A municipal public library is a chartered educational institution under NYS Education Law. Library funds remain library funds even when held by the municipality for custody or accounting purposes.

**Main risks to consider:**

- Municipal overreach into library governance or spending decisions.
- Confusion between General Municipal Law requirements and Education Law authority.
- Improper constraints placed on library reserve access, including unnecessary public hearings or referenda.

**Actionable options:**

- Clarify, in writing, that funds held by the municipality are library funds, subject to board resolution, not municipal discretion.
- Use board resolutions, not municipal approvals, to authorize use of library reserves. Municipalities execute transfers but do not approve them.
- If confusion persists, pursue a written agreement or memorandum of understanding that defines:
  - Custody versus control of funds.
  - Library authority under Education Law.
  - Municipal administrative responsibilities only.
- Ensure the library has independent legal representation if negotiating agreements that affect governance or finances.
