Procurement:
Getting It Right
the First Time

Public libraries spend public money. That means procurement must be transparent, fair, and documented.

Core Principles

The Foundation of
Sound Procurement

  • Spend prudently. Always aim for quality at the lowest responsible cost.
  • Promote fairness. Guard against favoritism, fraud, and waste.
  • Document everything. If it isn't written down, it didn't happen.
  • Follow the law. Especially General Municipal Law §§103 and 104-b.
Competitive Bidding — GML §103

Know Your Thresholds

$20,000+
Purchase contracts must go to public competitive bid.
$35,000+
Public works contracts must go to public competitive bid.
Below-threshold purchases
Governed by your board-adopted procurement policy. Document your method regardless of dollar amount.
Bidding Exceptions

When Competitive Bidding
Is Not Required

  • Professional services (auditors, attorneys, consultants): Use RFPs to ensure fairness.
  • OGS State Contracts: Libraries may purchase directly from approved vendors.
  • Single source: Only when it is provable that one vendor can supply the item or service.
  • Small-dollar purchases: Follow your local procurement policy. Require quotes at lower thresholds.
InsightEvery exception still requires written documentation. "We always use them" is not a justification.
Internal Policy — GML §104-b

What Your Procurement
Policy Must Include

  • Explains how staff determine whether bidding is required.
  • Requires quotes or alternative pricing for purchases below thresholds.
  • Demands written justification when the lowest price is not chosen.
  • Is reviewed at least once every five years.
  • Is publicly available.
Sample Policy — Non-Biddable Procurements

Dollar Tier Framework

  • $0–$2,500: At the discretion of the Director.
  • $2,501–$9,999: Document at least two verbal or written quotes.
  • $10,000–$19,999 (purchases) / $10,000–$34,999 (public works): Document at least three written quotes, proposals, or price comparisons.
Important: Board Approval Still RequiredEven when the Director has authority to approve a purchase at the lowest tier, the Board of Trustees must still approve all claims before funds are disbursed. This is a common point of confusion. The Director can initiate the purchase. Only the board can authorize the payment.
Best Practices

Staying Compliant
and Audit-Ready

  • Use cooperative bidding and piggyback contracts when available.
  • Keep procurement files organized: RFPs, quotes, bid tabulations, board approvals.
  • Have the board approve large purchases before contracts are signed.
  • Train staff on when they can and cannot make purchases independently.
  • Review your procurement policy, investment policy, and reserve policy annually alongside budget planning. Doing this at the annual meeting creates a clear, consistent record and makes tracking easier.

Audits:
Making OSC
Work for You

The Office of the State Comptroller audits public libraries regularly. The libraries that fare best treat audits as a governance tool, not a threat.

01
Top Audit Hotspot

Claims Audit Process

The board must review and approve all claims with supporting documentation before payment is released. That approval must appear in the minutes.

- The board cannot rubber-stamp a stack of claims. Each should be reviewed with invoices attached.
- Some claims may be paid prior to audit under limited circumstances (utilities, payroll), but the board must ratify them at the next meeting.
- A missing or incomplete claims audit is one of the most common findings OSC issues to libraries.

02
Top Audit Hotspot

Board Oversight

Trustees must monitor finances, review reports, enforce policies, and document decisions. A passive board is an audit finding waiting to happen.

- Board minutes must accurately reflect what was discussed, reviewed, and decided. Vague minutes that say "financials reviewed" without detail are a red flag.
- The board should ask questions about financial reports. Silent approval without inquiry signals inadequate oversight.
- OSC looks for evidence that trustees are engaged, not merely present.

03
Top Audit Hotspot

Cash Receipts Management

What this means: Cash receipts management covers every dollar coming into the library: fines, fees, program revenue, donations, and tax receipts. OSC looks at whether money is recorded at the point of collection, deposited intact and promptly, and handled by more than one person.

- Duties must be segregated: the person who receives cash should not be the same person who records or deposits it.
- All receipts should be documented with a pre-numbered receipt or logged in a register.
- Deposits should be made promptly and intact. Holding cash to accumulate a larger deposit is a control gap.

04
Top Audit Hotspot

Procurement Policies and Practices

Libraries must comply with competitive bidding laws and their own board-adopted procurement policy. OSC will check both.

- OSC auditors will pull purchase records and verify that quotes were obtained at the required thresholds.
- They will also check whether the policy itself exists, is current, and has been formally adopted by the board.
- Professional services (attorneys, auditors, IT consultants) must still be solicited competitively via RFP even though they are exempt from formal bidding.

05
Top Audit Hotspot

Treasurer's Role and Custody of Funds

The Treasurer must retain custody of funds, sign checks, and issue financial reports. Delegating these duties wholesale is a recurring audit finding.

- Under Education Law §259, the Treasurer holds library funds and is accountable for them. The role is not honorary.
- The Treasurer should sign (or co-sign) checks. Using a signature stamp without adequate oversight is cited regularly by OSC.
- Monthly financial reports must come from the Treasurer to the board, not from the Director or bookkeeper alone.
- The Treasurer must also file an annual financial report with OSC under General Municipal Law §30(3).

06
Top Audit Hotspot

Fund Balance and Budgeting

Budgets must be realistic. Surplus funds must have a documented plan for use. An unexplained, growing fund balance draws scrutiny.

- OSC recognizes that libraries may legally accumulate surplus funds and carry them forward year to year (Opinion 2002-7). What triggers a finding is having no plan for the funds.
- Libraries that consistently budget conservatively and accumulate large reserves without a documented capital or reserve plan will be cited.
- A formal reserve policy with stated purposes and board authorization is the clearest way to protect surplus funds from audit scrutiny.

07
Top Audit Hotspot

Credit Card Usage

Policies must define authorized users, spending limits, and required documentation.

- Credit card policies must be board-adopted and name who is authorized to use library cards, for what purposes, and up to what dollar amount.
- Every charge requires an original receipt and a description of the business purpose. Missing receipts are among the most common credit card findings.
- Cards should not be shared informally among staff. Each card should have a named, accountable user.
- Online banking access tied to card accounts should also be addressed in policy.

08
Top Audit Hotspot

Bank Reconciliations

What a bank reconciliation is: A monthly comparison between the library's own accounting records and the bank's records. It confirms that every transaction in the checkbook matches what the bank shows, and flags discrepancies, missing deposits, or unauthorized withdrawals.

- Reconciliations must be completed monthly, reviewed by someone other than the preparer, and reported to the board.
- If the same person prepares and reviews the reconciliation, there is no independent check. OSC cites this consistently.
- The board should see and formally accept the reconciliation as part of its regular financial review.

09
Top Audit Hotspot

Investments

Funds must follow statutory investment rules (General Municipal Law §11) and a written, board-adopted investment policy.

- Libraries may invest in time deposit accounts, certificates of deposit, and certain U.S. or New York State obligations. Money market mutual funds and savings banks are not authorized.
- The board must authorize the investment program in writing. The chief fiscal officer carries it out.
- Idle cash sitting in a non-interest-bearing account is a finding.

"Failing to invest idle cash is equally as costly as paying an excess amount for a commodity, entering into an unneeded contract for services or issuing unnecessary debt."OSC, Investing and Protecting Public Funds — Local Government Management Guide
10
Top Audit Hotspot

Payroll and Personnel Controls

Pay rates, overtime, and benefits must match board-approved schedules or contracts. Payroll must be reviewed by someone other than the person who processes it.

- The board must formally adopt a salary schedule or approve individual compensation. Pay that deviates from board-approved rates is a finding.
- Timesheets must be completed, signed, and reviewed. Overtime must be pre-authorized.
- If the Director also processes payroll, a second reviewer should verify it before submission.
- Leave accrual records must be maintained and reconciled against usage. OSC regularly audits leave payout practices at separation.

11
Top Audit Hotspot

IT Security and Data Protection

Libraries need up-to-date IT policies, secure access controls, backup procedures, and a documented cyber incident plan. OSC has been citing this area with increasing frequency.

- User accounts should be tied to individual employees, not shared. Access should be revoked promptly when staff leave.
- Data must be backed up regularly, with backups stored offsite or in the cloud.
- A written acceptable use policy and cybersecurity incident response plan are baseline expectations.
- Online banking access is a specific sub-area. Who can initiate, who can approve, and what dollar limits apply must all be defined in policy.

12
Top Audit Hotspot

Capital Assets and Inventory

Equipment, technology, and furniture must be inventoried, tagged, and tracked. Disposals require board oversight and documentation.

- Libraries must maintain a written inventory of capital assets. Items should be tagged and periodically verified against the list.
- When assets are disposed of, the board must authorize the disposal and document the method (donated, sold, discarded).
- Technology assets are the area most commonly cited. Laptops, tablets, and servers that are unaccounted for are a control gap.
- Libraries should establish a capitalization threshold in policy (e.g., items over $500 are capitalized and tracked).

13
Top Audit Hotspot

Grants and Restricted Funds

Grant and restricted funds must be accounted for separately and spent only for their intended purposes.

- Commingling grant funds with the general operating fund is a serious finding. Each restricted fund should have its own account or be tracked separately in the accounting records.
- Grant expenditures must align with the approved grant budget. Spending outside approved categories requires funder approval before it occurs.
- Reporting deadlines must be met. OSC will check whether required interim and final reports were filed on time.
- Unspent grant funds must be returned or re-authorized. They cannot be absorbed into the operating budget.

14
Top Audit Hotspot

Cash Disbursements

Invoices should be paid timely, supported with documentation, and checked for duplication. This goes beyond the claims audit to include disbursement controls generally.

- Every payment should be supported by an original invoice, not a statement or estimate.
- Duplicate payment checks — same vendor, same amount, same period — should be flagged and reviewed before payment.
- Payments to individuals (honoraria, stipends, contractors) require special scrutiny: employee vs. contractor status, 1099 obligations, and board approval of the amount.
- Direct bank transfers and ACH payments require the same documentation as checks and must be included in the claims audit.

15
Top Audit Hotspot

Open Meetings and Transparency

Board decisions must be taken in open session. Minutes must accurately reflect actions taken.

- Executive session is permitted only for specific statutory purposes under the Open Meetings Law. Using it to avoid public discussion of financial matters is a violation.
- Minutes must include motions, votes, and the names of who moved and seconded. "Discussion ensued" is not an adequate record.
- Board meeting agendas should be posted in advance. Supporting materials (financial reports, claims lists) should be available to the public on request.
- Decisions made informally between meetings via email or phone, then ratified without full discussion in open session, are a recurring finding.

Common Audit Red Flags

Address These Before OSC Does

  • Payments made before board approval.
  • Procurement done with no documentation, or only a phone call to a vendor.
  • Treasurer not signing checks or issuing monthly reports.
  • No written credit card policy, or missing receipts.
  • Board not receiving or reviewing bank reconciliations.
  • No written IT, cybersecurity, or online banking policy.
  • Fund balances growing with no documented plan or reserve policy.
  • No inventory of technology and equipment.
  • No investment policy, or idle funds left uninvested in a non-interest-bearing account.
  • Director processing payroll with no independent review.
  • Leave accruals not tracked or not reconciled at separation.
  • Grant or restricted funds commingled with operating funds.
  • No written conflict of interest or ethics policy for board members.
  • Executive session used for purposes not authorized by the Open Meetings Law.
  • ACH or online payments not included in the claims audit.
  • Procurement policy not reviewed or re-adopted in more than five years.
  • Annual financial report not filed with OSC on time under GML §30(3).

Strategic Perspective

Using OSC Audits
to Your Advantage

  • Study past audits. Use OSC's online audit search to see what findings other libraries face.
  • Benchmark yourself. Compare your policies and practices against OSC recommendations.
  • Fix proactively. If OSC regularly cites libraries for weak cash handling, address yours before an audit arrives.
  • Use it as a transparency tool. Share your strong financial controls with taxpayers, donors, and local officials.
  • Justify policy updates. OSC audit findings give you concrete grounds to bring policy revisions to your board.
Key Takeaways

What to Carry
Back to Your Library

  • Procurement is about fairness, compliance, and documentation. All three are required.
  • OSC audits focus on governance, transparency, and internal controls. If your board is engaged, you are already ahead.
  • A board that actively reviews, questions, and documents its decisions is well on its way to being audit-ready.
InsightThe single most protective thing a board can do is show up, ask questions, and put decisions in the minutes. Passive boards generate audit findings. Active boards prevent them.
