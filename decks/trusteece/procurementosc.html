Procurement:
Getting It Right
the First Time

Public libraries spend public money. That means procurement must be transparent, fair, and documented.

Core Principles

The Foundation of
Sound Procurement

  • Spend prudently. Always aim for quality at the lowest responsible cost.
  • Promote fairness. Guard against favoritism, fraud, and waste.
  • Document everything. If it isn't written down, it didn't happen.
  • Follow the law. Especially General Municipal Law §§103 and 104-b.
Competitive Bidding — GML §103

Know Your Thresholds

$20,000+
Purchase contracts must go to public competitive bid.
$35,000+
Public works contracts must go to public competitive bid.
Below-threshold purchases
Governed by your board-adopted procurement policy. Document your method regardless of dollar amount.
Bidding Exceptions

When Competitive Bidding
Is Not Required

  • Professional services (auditors, attorneys, consultants): Use RFPs to ensure fairness.
  • OGS State Contracts: Libraries may purchase directly from approved vendors.
  • Single source: Only when it is provable that one vendor can supply the item or service.
  • Small-dollar purchases: Follow your local procurement policy. Require quotes at lower thresholds.
InsightEvery exception still requires written documentation. "We always use them" is not a justification.
Internal Policy — GML §104-b

What Your Procurement
Policy Must Include

  • Explains how staff determine whether bidding is required.
  • Requires quotes or alternative pricing for purchases below thresholds.
  • Demands written justification when the lowest price is not chosen.
  • Is reviewed at least once every five years.
  • Is publicly available.
Sample Policy — Non-Biddable Procurements

Dollar Tier Framework

  • $0–$2,500: At the discretion of the Director.
  • $2,501–$9,999: Document at least two verbal or written quotes.
  • $10,000–$19,999 (purchases) / $10,000–$34,999 (public works): Document at least three written quotes, proposals, or price comparisons.
InsightThe board must still approve all claims before funds are disbursed, regardless of who authorized the purchase. For contracts or ongoing obligations, get board approval before signing.
Best Practices

Staying Compliant
and Audit-Ready

  • Use cooperative bidding and piggyback contracts when available.
  • Keep procurement files organized: RFPs, quotes, bid tabulations, board approvals.
  • Have the board approve large purchases before contracts are signed.
  • Train staff on when they can and cannot make purchases independently.
  • Review procurement policy annually alongside budget planning.

Audits:
Making OSC
Work for You

The Office of the State Comptroller audits public libraries regularly. The libraries that fare best treat audits as a governance tool, not a threat.

01
Top Audit Hotspot

Claims Audit Process

The board must review and approve all claims with supporting documentation. That approval must appear in the minutes. No approval, no payment.

02
Top Audit Hotspot

Board Oversight

Trustees must monitor finances, review reports, enforce policies, and document decisions. A passive board is an audit finding waiting to happen.

03
Top Audit Hotspot

Cash Receipts Management

All money must be receipted, deposited intact, and safeguarded. Duties should be segregated so no single person handles both receipt and recording.

04
Top Audit Hotspot

Procurement Policies and Practices

Libraries must comply with competitive bidding laws and their own board-adopted procurement policy. OSC will check both.

05
Top Audit Hotspot

Treasurer's Role and Custody of Funds

The Treasurer must retain custody of funds, sign checks, and issue financial reports to the board. Delegating these duties wholesale is a recurring audit finding.

06
Top Audit Hotspot

Fund Balance and Budgeting

Budgets must be realistic. Surplus funds must have a documented plan for use. An unexplained, growing fund balance draws scrutiny.

07
Top Audit Hotspot

Credit Card Usage

Policies must define authorized users, spending limits, and required documentation. Missing receipts and missing policies show up in nearly every audit where credit cards are in use.

08
Top Audit Hotspot

Bank Reconciliations

Reconciliations must be done monthly, reviewed independently, and reported to the board. If the person who prepares the reconciliation is also the one who reviews it, that is a control gap.

09
Top Audit Hotspot

Investments

Funds must follow statutory investment rules and a written investment policy. Libraries sitting on idle cash with no investment policy are regularly cited.

10
Top Audit Hotspot

Payroll and Personnel Controls

Pay rates, overtime, and benefits must match board-approved schedules or contracts. Payroll must be reviewed by someone other than the person who processes it.

11
Top Audit Hotspot

IT Security and Data Protection

Libraries need up-to-date IT policies, secure access controls, backup procedures, and a cyber incident plan. OSC has been citing this area with increasing frequency.

12
Top Audit Hotspot

Capital Assets and Inventory

Equipment, technology, and furniture must be inventoried, tagged, and tracked. Disposals require board oversight and documentation.

13
Top Audit Hotspot

Grants and Restricted Funds

Grant and restricted funds must be accounted for separately and spent only for their intended purposes. Commingling funds is a serious finding.

14
Top Audit Hotspot

Cash Disbursements

Invoices should be paid timely, supported with documentation, and checked for duplication. This goes beyond the claims audit to include disbursement controls generally.

15
Top Audit Hotspot

Open Meetings and Transparency

Board decisions must be taken in open session. Minutes must accurately reflect actions taken. Executive session misuse and incomplete minutes are common findings.

Common Audit Red Flags

Address These Before OSC Does

  • Payments are being made before board approval.
  • Procurement done with no documentation or only a phone call to a vendor.
  • Treasurer not signing checks or issuing monthly reports.
  • No written credit card policy, or missing receipts.
  • Board not receiving or reviewing bank reconciliations.
  • No written IT or cybersecurity policy.
  • Fund balances growing with no documented plan.
  • No inventory list of technology and equipment.
  • No investment policy, or assets not being invested appropriately.

Who Does
What

Audit findings often come down to unclear roles. Here is the division of responsibility that OSC expects to see.

Roles and Responsibilities

Board of Trustees vs. Library Director

Strategic Perspective

Using OSC Audits
to Your Advantage

  • Study past audits. Use OSC's online audit search to see what findings other libraries face.
  • Benchmark yourself. Compare your policies and practices against OSC recommendations.
  • Fix proactively. If OSC regularly cites libraries for weak cash handling, address yours before an audit arrives.
  • Use it as a transparency tool. Share your strong financial controls with taxpayers, donors, and local officials.
  • Justify policy updates. OSC audit findings give you concrete grounds to bring policy revisions to your board.
Key Takeaways

What to Carry
Back to Your Library

  • Procurement is about fairness, compliance, and documentation. All three are required.
  • OSC audits focus on governance, transparency, and internal controls. If your board is engaged, you are already ahead.
  • A board that actively reviews, questions, and documents its decisions is well on its way to being audit-ready.
InsightThe single most protective thing a board can do is show up, ask questions, and put decisions in the minutes. Passive boards generate audit findings. Active boards prevent them.
