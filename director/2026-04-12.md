## Photography, Recording, and Patron Consent

Last week's briefing addressed two legal concerns that arise when library staff post photos on social media: images that inadvertently expose patron library card numbers, which may implicate both New York CPLR 4509 and the OWWL Systems Access Policy, and photos that reveal a patron's presence in the library, which is protected information that should not be shared publicly without consent. (As noted last week, the second concern rests on legal interpretation and policy guidance rather than the explicit text of the statute.)

In response to several questions from libraries, the Photography and Recording Policy has been updated with input from directors. The revised policy, along with a photo release form for obtaining patron consent, is available here:

[Photography and Recording Policy (Word Document)](https://docs.owwl.org/pub/Community/PolicyLibrary/Photography%20and%20Recording%20Policy.docx)

Beyond compliance, this is also a good moment to think more broadly about how your library uses patron photos and what purposes they serve. Libraries that approach photography with intention, deciding in advance what kinds of images they want, for what audiences, and for which platforms, tend to produce stronger promotional content. A little planning at the front end makes a real difference in the quality and usefulness of what you capture.

### System Direct Access Plan 2027–2031: Open for Comment

**Deadline: April 30, 2026**

OWWL is seeking feedback from member library directors and boards on the draft Direct Access Plan for 2027–2031 before it moves forward for formal approval.

**What is the Direct Access Plan?** It is a required component of the OWWL System's Plan of Service. It governs how library services are made available to all residents within the System's boundaries, including those living outside any member library's chartered service area. It also outlines the conditions under which member libraries may request modifications to unrestricted direct access.

**What's new in this draft.** This draft includes several updates from the current 2022–2026 plan:

- A new negotiation procedure that requires the System to work with municipalities on funding solutions before any restrictions can be imposed
- Updated language affirming the System's role in supporting equitable access
- A new commitment to produce a report identifying unserved areas across Ontario, Wayne, Wyoming, and Livingston Counties

Directors and boards are asked to review the draft and share feedback. The current 2022–2026 plan is available for comparison.

> **Note:** No board action is needed at this time. Once feedback has been reviewed and incorporated, the OWWL Board of Trustees will approve the plan. Following that, a majority of member library boards will need to approve the plan before submission to the Division of Library Development. Separate instructions will follow when that step is ready.

Questions? Contact Ron at [rkirsop@owwl.org](mailto:rkirsop@owwl.org).

## This Week in OSC Audits

Five audits this week. The theme is consistent: people in charge of money did not keep track of the money.

[A city](https://www.osc.ny.gov/local-government/audits/city/2026/03/27/city-ithaca-records-and-reports-2025m-114) did not file its annual financial reports for 2022, 2023, or 2024. Its most recently completed independent audit covers 2021. Several bank accounts holding a combined $51 million were not reconciled on time. The governing body went years without receiving basic monthly financial reports. To be fair, officials are now actively working to fix this.

[A town supervisor](https://www.osc.ny.gov/local-government/audits/town/2026/03/27/town-cato-supervisors-records-and-reports-2025m-141) delegated the recordkeeping and then did not check the records. Hundreds of thousands of dollars in transactions were not documented. Annual financial reports are missing going back years.

[A regional educational services organization](https://www.osc.ny.gov/local-government/audits/boces/2026/03/20/monroe-2-orleans-board-cooperative-educational-services-mifi-usage-billings-2025m) paid roughly $28,800 in unnecessary charges for 62 mobile devices that were inactive or no longer needed. No one was watching the bill.

[A second town](https://www.osc.ny.gov/local-government/audits/statewide-audit/2026/03/20/town-brant-transparency-fiscal-activities-s9-25-23) did not conduct its required annual audit of the supervisor's financial records, did not file three years of annual financial reports, and did not give the board reliable monthly reports. The board cannot oversee what it cannot see.

[A school district](https://www.osc.ny.gov/local-government/audits/school-district/2026/03/20/chittenango-central-school-district-audit-follow-2023m-155-f) was revisited on an IT audit from 2024. Four recommendations were made. Two were partially implemented. Two were not implemented. Progress is progress.

None of this starts with bad intentions. It starts with no one assigned to check, no one following up, and policies that exist on paper but not in practice.

### So Your Library Doesn't End Up in One of These

The same problems show up in audit after audit. Here is what actually prevents them.

Assign someone to reconcile accounts every month, and make sure a second person reviews it. Not because you don't trust the first person, but because errors are easier to catch when two sets of eyes are on the numbers.

File your annual financial report on time. It is a legal requirement, and falling behind creates a backlog that is much harder to dig out of than it is to avoid.

Give your board real financial reports every month. Budget-to-actual comparisons, cash balances, fund balance estimates. Trustees cannot ask good questions about information they have never seen.

Review your vendor bills. Subscriptions, service contracts, devices. Things that get set up and forgotten tend to keep generating charges long after they stop being useful.

When an auditor or reviewer makes a recommendation, assign it to someone with a deadline. The follow-up audit will notice.
