## Self-Checkout Receipt Privacy Change

A member library raised a concern that the system-wide self-checkout receipt template prints the patron's name alongside the items checked out. A receipt that links a named patron to specific items is a confidential library record under Civil Practice Law and Rules §4509 (CPLR 4509). A receipt left at a self-checkout machine or used as a bookmark exposes that record.

In consultation with the Evergreen Advisory Committee, the system is removing the patron's name and other identifying information from the system-wide self-checkout receipt template. This does not change what patrons can do at self-checkout.

CPLR 4509 applies to all library records that identify a patron in connection with materials they have used. Directors should treat this change as a patron privacy compliance matter, not only a workflow adjustment.

We strongly encourage every library to review its own circulation receipt templates for any patron identifiers. Where an identifier is useful — for example, to help family members keep their items straight — we recommend truncating it to the last four digits rather than printing a full name or full barcode number. Printing a full barcode number is a particular risk, since it can be used to borrow items on another patron's account.

If removing this information disrupts your workflow, open a support ticket and we will work through alternatives with you. We are also glad to help you review your templates to identify patron-identifying fields.

This change will be reviewed at the September OWWLDAC meeting under the EAC item.

## Systems Access and Email Training

The September 30 deadline for completing the Systems Access and Email Training requirement is approaching. If your library has not made this a priority, now is the time.

At its May 13, 2026 meeting, the OWWL Board of Trustees adopted a resolution requiring all member library staff and volunteers who access OWWL systems to complete two training courses. The full resolution is available in the [May 13, 2026 board minutes](https://owwl.org/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/2026.05.13-System-Board-Minutes.pdf#page=4).

The two required courses are:

**Systems Access Training** covers acceptable use of OWWL systems, including Evergreen, network resources, and shared tools. This course applies to anyone who logs into OWWL-managed systems.

**Email Security Training** covers how to recognize phishing attempts, handle suspicious messages, and protect your library's accounts. This course applies to anyone who uses email as part of their library role.

Both courses are self-paced and available through the OWWL learning platform. Directors are responsible for ensuring that every eligible staff member and volunteer at their library completes both courses by September 30, 2026.

If you have questions about who is required to complete the training, or if you run into any issues with access, open a support ticket and our team will assist you.

## Question of the Week: Why Structure Matters for Board Motions

**Does it matter how a board records its motions, or is capturing the general intention enough?**

Every decision a library board makes must begin with a motion and end with a vote. That is not a formality. It is the mechanism through which a board acts as a legal governing body.

Under New York State General Construction Law §41, no action can be approved by the board of a public library without a majority of the whole number of trustees, regardless of how many are present at the meeting. A conversation, a consensus, a note in the minutes — none of these constitute a board decision. Only a vote does.

**Why the structure matters**

The board acts [collectively](https://nyslibrary.libguides.com/Handbook-Library-Trustees/board-organization#:~:text=accountability%20and%20transparency.-,Collective%20Authority,-Governance%20authority%20and). As the Handbook for Library Trustees of New York State states, individual trustees have no authority to speak or act on behalf of the library unless the board has specifically granted that authority through a vote. That collective authority only comes into being when a motion is made, seconded, and voted on. Without that sequence, nothing has happened in a legal sense.

This matters for several reasons. When a director implements something the board "agreed to" in conversation but never actually voted on, there is no board authorization behind the action. If the decision is later questioned — whether by a trustee, an auditor, or a community member — there is nothing in the record to point to. The board's minutes are required by law to record "all motions, proposals, resolutions, and any other matter formally voted upon and the vote thereon." A note summarizing what the board discussed, or what the consensus seemed to be, does not meet that standard.

**What a weak motion looks like**

Here is an example of a motion that creates problems.

> "I move that we do something about the staffing situation."

This motion names no position, no salary, no start date, and no authorization for the director to act. If it passes, what exactly did the board approve? The answer is nothing that can be implemented, defended, or documented. Vague motions produce vague records, and vague records produce governance gaps.

**What a well-formed motion looks like**

A good motion is specific enough that anyone reading the minutes six months later knows exactly what was decided.

> "MOTION: [TRUSTEE FIRST AND LAST NAME] moves to create a part-time Library Clerk position at a salary of $16.00 per hour, not to exceed 19 hours per week, and to authorize the Director to proceed with the Ontario County Civil Service classification process and subsequent recruitment. [TRUSTEE FIRST AND LAST NAME] seconds and the motion passed unanimously."

This motion identifies the position title, the compensation, the hours, and the specific authority being granted to the director. The board has a clear, documented record of what it approved. The director has clear authorization to act. And if the decision is ever reviewed, the minutes will show exactly what the board did. This is true for all actions of the board.

**A few practical reminders**

A motion is not a decision until it is voted on. Nodding around the table, agreeing by consensus, or having the board president say "we'll do that" are not votes. If something is worth doing, it is worth a motion and a vote.

Adding a note to the minutes that says "the board discussed expanding hours" or "trustees agreed the budget needed review" is not board action. It is a record of a conversation. Until a motion is made and a vote is taken, the board has not acted.

Before your next board meeting, it is worth pulling up your recent minutes and asking whether the motions recorded there are specific enough to stand on their own. If someone unfamiliar with your library read those minutes, would they know exactly what was authorized? If not, that is worth addressing.

I am happy to review board meeting minutes to offer suggestions if you'd like. Just send me a couple of links and I'll put together recommendations.

## This Week in OSC Audits

Below is a summary of recent Office of the State Comptroller audits. These audits examine towns, villages, and other local entities across New York. The settings vary, but the themes are consistent ones for any public institution, including libraries.

For directors and trustees, these audits are useful reminders about where governance systems tend to break down and what practices help maintain public trust.

### [A village — Records and Reports](https://www.osc.ny.gov/local-government/audits/village/2026/06/12/village-whitehall-records-and-reports-2026m-9)

**Issues identified**

- The clerk-treasurer did not maintain complete or accurate accounting records and did not provide the board with required monthly financial reports.
- The Annual Financial Report was not filed.
- Asset and liability accounts contained significant errors, with general fund liabilities understated by over $700,000 and fund balance potentially overstated by more than $870,000.
- Bank reconciliations were missing or incomplete for most of the audit period.
- The board never conducted the required annual audit of the clerk-treasurer's records.

**Takeaways for libraries**

- Boards are responsible for reviewing financial reports regularly, not annually. If the director is not presenting monthly financial information, ask for it.
- The board's annual audit of the treasurer's records is a legal requirement, not an optional practice. It should appear on the calendar every year.
- Errors in fund balance reporting can have real consequences for budgeting and compliance. Boards should ask enough questions to catch significant discrepancies before an outside auditor does.

### [A town — Transparency of Fiscal Activities](https://www.osc.ny.gov/local-government/audits/statewide-audit/2026/06/12/town-lewis-transparency-fiscal-activities-s9-25-31)

**Issues identified**

- Only two board members audited the supervisor's financial records rather than the full board, as required, and no documentation of that audit was kept.
- Cash receipt and payroll issues went undetected as a result.
- The supervisor failed to file three consecutive years of Annual Financial Reports.

**Takeaways for libraries**

- The claims audit process belongs to the full board, not a subset of members acting informally. If your library delegates this to a finance committee, make sure the process is documented and reported back to the full board.
- Filing the Annual Financial Report is a compliance requirement with real consequences for libraries, including loss of registration and funding eligibility. Directors should flag any filing concerns to the board well before a deadline.

### [A second town — Transparency of Fiscal Activities](https://www.osc.ny.gov/local-government/audits/statewide-audit/2026/06/12/town-union-transparency-fiscal-activities-s9-25-40)

**Issues identified**

- The board did not conduct a timely annual audit of the town's financial records.
- The comptroller failed to file the Annual Financial Report.
- The board did not receive complete or reliable monthly financial reports.
- Bank reconciliations were not performed due to numerous general ledger errors.

**Takeaways for libraries**

- Reliable financial reporting depends on accurate underlying records. If reconciliations are not being done, financial reports cannot be trusted. Boards should ask whether reconciliations are current.
- When a board is not receiving complete financial information, it cannot exercise meaningful oversight. Directors should ensure that what they present to the board is accurate enough to support real decision-making.

### [A third town — Host Fee Payments](https://www.osc.ny.gov/local-government/audits/town/2026/06/18/town-schuyler-falls-host-fee-payments-2026m-26)

**Issues identified**

- A landfill operator significantly miscalculated quarterly host fee payments over nearly a decade.
- Of 39 payments reviewed, 35 contained errors totaling over $460,000, leaving the municipality short nearly $330,000.
- The town had no procedure to verify that payments were calculated correctly.

**Takeaways for libraries**

- Any recurring payment or revenue source your library receives — whether from a municipality, a contract, or a grant — should have a verification step. Receiving a check is not the same as confirming the amount is correct.
- The absence of a verification procedure meant errors compounded for years before anyone caught them. Boards should ask whether revenue sources are being monitored, not only received.

> Across all of these audits, a consistent pattern appears. Problems rarely begin with fraud or misconduct. They begin with small gaps in oversight, unclear documentation, or policies that drift out of alignment with actual practice. Regular review of financial procedures, procurement policies, and board reporting practices helps prevent those small gaps from becoming larger governance failures.
