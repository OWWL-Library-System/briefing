## Upcoming: Minimum Standards Webinar - Selection and Reconsideration Policies

This Friday, June 12, from 1:00 to 2:00 PM, the New York State Library is hosting the next session in its webinar series on the new [Minimum Standards for Public and Association Libraries](https://nyslibrary.libguides.com/publiclibrarystandards/policies). This session covers selection and reconsideration policies, both required elements under the new standards.

The session will address the current climate of heightened scrutiny around library materials, the ethical obligations of library workers to uphold professional ethics, and the foundations of intellectual freedom, including the First Amendment and ALA's Library Bill of Rights and Freedom to Read Statement. Presenters will also walk through what a strong materials selection policy should include, how to address AI-generated materials, and how to structure a reconsideration policy that is clear, consistent, and defensible.

All webinars in this series are recorded. To receive the recording link, you must register in advance.

Questions about this session can be directed to Tor Loney at [tor.loney@nysed.gov](mailto:tor.loney@nysed.gov).

## This Week in OSC Audits

Below is a summary of recent Office of the State Comptroller audits. These audits examine towns and villages across New York. The settings vary, but the themes are consistent ones for any public institution, including libraries.

For directors and trustees, these audits are useful reminders about where governance systems tend to break down and what practices help maintain public trust.

### Five Towns: Transparency of Fiscal Activities

Auditors reviewed five towns across New York for the same set of findings. In each case, the board did not conduct or provide for an annual audit of the supervisor's financial records as required by Town Law, the supervisor did not file Annual Financial Reports with the State Comptroller as required by General Municipal Law, and the board did not receive complete, accurate, or timely monthly financial reports. These audits were issued for [a town in Schuyler County](https://www.osc.ny.gov/local-government/audits/statewide-audit/2026/05/15/town-catharine-transparency-fiscal-activities-s9-25-26), [a town in Steuben County](https://www.osc.ny.gov/local-government/audits/statewide-audit/2026/05/08/town-fremont-transparency-fiscal-activities-s9-25-29), [a town in Greene County](https://www.osc.ny.gov/local-government/audits/statewide-audit/2026/05/15/town-greenville-transparency-fiscal-activities-s9-25-41), [a town in Allegany County](https://www.osc.ny.gov/local-government/audits/statewide-audit/2026/05/15/town-new-hudson-transparency-fiscal-activities-s9-25-34), and [a town in Jefferson County](https://www.osc.ny.gov/local-government/audits/statewide-audit/2026/05/08/town-pamelia-transparency-fiscal-activities-s9-25-35).

**Takeaways for libraries**

- Town Law requires an annual audit of the supervisor's records. The library equivalent is the board's responsibility to review and approve the treasurer's report at each meeting and to ensure an independent audit is conducted annually.
- Filing Annual Financial Reports is a statutory obligation. Libraries have parallel reporting requirements, and boards should confirm those filings are current.
- When boards receive incomplete or inaccurate financial information month after month, they cannot make informed decisions. Directors should present financial reports that give trustees enough detail to exercise genuine oversight.

### [A Town: Procurement](https://www.osc.ny.gov/local-government/audits/town/2026/05/08/town-spencer-procurement-2025m-100)

A town was audited for its procurement practices. Auditors found that the town did not consistently follow required competitive procurement procedures, and some purchases were made without adequate documentation or board-level review.

**Takeaways for libraries**

- New York's competitive bidding thresholds apply to libraries. Directors should know those dollar thresholds and apply them consistently, not selectively.
- Procurement decisions that bypass competition, even when the outcome seems reasonable, create audit exposure. Consistent documentation protects the board and the director.

### [A Village: Electric Utility Services - Billing and Collections](https://www.osc.ny.gov/local-government/audits/village/2026/05/08/village-churchville-electric-utility-services-billing-and-collections-2026m-5)

A village that operates a municipal electric utility was audited for its billing and collections practices. Auditors found weaknesses in how the utility tracked accounts, applied payments, and followed up on delinquent balances, leaving revenue unprotected and records unreliable.

**Takeaways for libraries**

- Libraries that collect fees, fines, or other revenue need clear procedures for tracking what is owed, recording what is received, and following up on unpaid amounts.
- Board members should periodically ask how revenue collections are tracked and whether reconciliation happens regularly. Gaps in this area are easy to overlook until they become significant.

### [A Village: Financial Condition](https://www.osc.ny.gov/local-government/audits/village/2026/05/08/village-fredonia-financial-condition-2025m-149)

A village was audited for its financial condition after years of declining fund balance. Auditors found that the board did not fully understand the village's fiscal position and relied heavily on the treasurer for guidance rather than exercising independent oversight. The board repeatedly adopted budgets with planned operating deficits, drawing down fund balance across three operating funds over six years. The village ultimately had to issue a revenue anticipation note to cover operations, and when that note came due the following year, repaying it required a 55 percent property tax increase.

**Takeaways for libraries**

- Boards that rely entirely on the treasurer or director to interpret financial information are not exercising genuine oversight. Trustees should be able to read a budget-to-actual report and ask informed questions about what they see.
- Adopting a budget with a planned deficit is a choice with consequences. Libraries should have a written fund balance policy that defines acceptable levels and guides how fund balance is used or preserved.
- A multi-year financial plan helps boards see where current decisions lead. Without one, each year's budget feels self-contained even when the cumulative trend is heading in a difficult direction.

### [A Village: Claims Auditing](https://www.osc.ny.gov/local-government/audits/village/2026/05/15/village-rhinebeck-claims-auditing-2025m-147)

A village was audited for its claims auditing practices. Auditors found that the board did not conduct a thorough review of claims before authorizing payment, and some payments were made without adequate supporting documentation.

**Takeaways for libraries**

- Approving a list of claims at a board meeting is not a formality. Trustees should have access to supporting documentation and be willing to ask questions about any item that is unclear.
- A claims audit process that is documented and consistent is one of the most basic protections a board has against improper payments.

> Across all of these audits, a consistent pattern appears. Problems rarely begin with fraud or misconduct. They begin with small gaps in oversight, unclear documentation, or policies that drift out of alignment with actual practice. Regular review of financial procedures, procurement policies, and board reporting practices helps prevent those small gaps from becoming larger governance failures.
