## Systems Access and Email Security Training: Board Action Tomorrow

At tomorrow evening's OWWL Library System Board of Trustees meeting, the Board will take two actions related to data security and patron privacy.

First, the Board will adopt an update to the Systems Access and Confidentiality of Library Records Policy that sets the requirement in place for the security training of new authorized users. Second, the Board will pass a resolution requiring all current holders of OWWL email accounts and System accounts who have not previously completed the required "Systems Access and Confidentiality of Library Records" and "Email Security Training" courses to do so by September 30, 2026.

Both of these items have been discussed at OWWLDAC, including at the March 6 and May 1 meetings, and directors have had the opportunity to weigh in on the approach. The resolution will go out to all directors on Thursday, May 14. A draft memo that directors can use to communicate the training requirement to their staff is attached to the May 1 OWWLDAC Agenda. [Here is the link](https://docs.owwl.org/pub/Members/Meetings/DirectorsAdvisoryCouncilAgendaMay12026/2026.05.01%20Draft%20Memo%20Requested%20by%20OWWLDAC%20for%20Systems%20Access%20Training.docx).

The training courses are brief, approximately thirty minutes each, and are available through the Staff Academy on Niche. Failure to complete both trainings by September 30, 2026 will result in a password reset for associated OWWL email and System accounts. Password resets under this resolution are security measures, not disciplinary actions.

If an account holder misses the deadline, account access will not be restored without a written request to me that includes a justification for the missed deadline, documentation that the account holder has signed the local Systems Access and Confidentiality of Library Records Acknowledgment Form, and confirmation that all current Authorized Users at the library have been reviewed for compliance.

More detail follows on Thursday. Questions are welcome in the meantime.

## Candidate Profiles and the Line Libraries Cannot Cross

> **Short version**: Check your library's communication about Trustee Candidates. Several libraries have been posting information about upcoming elections that could be viewed as endorsing a candidate, which is not allowed. A library may post the name of a candidate, years in the district, and the term being sought. Anything more could be problematic.  

Several libraries have been sharing candidate profiles on their official platforms during trustee elections. These posts have included candidate photographs, personal biographies, and personal statements. When the question arose of whether this practice is appropriate, the answer was: probably not.

The governing legal standard comes from *Phillips v. Maurer*, 67 NY2d 672 (1986), which the NYS Commissioner of Education has applied directly to school district public libraries. A public entity may share objective, factual information about a vote or election, but may not distribute materials designed to advocate for a particular candidate or outcome. This standard is grounded in NYS Constitution Article VIII, Section 1, which bars school districts and associated entities from using public funds for partisan purposes.

OWWL consulted with legal counsel, and the guidance was direct. There is no bright line here, so the wise approach is to avoid getting near the line at all.

On candidate profiles, the recommended limit for anything posted on library platforms is name, years residing in the district, and the term being sought. Even opening the door to candidate statements carries risk. A candidate could submit something the library finds objectionable, and declining to post it creates a separate First Amendment problem. Posting biographical content creates exposure if any of it turns out to be inaccurate.

On social media comments, counsel recommends disabling comments on election-related posts before they become a problem. Removing comments after the fact creates its own First Amendment issues.

On petition gathering, the library should not be involved in any way. Even designating space in the building for all candidates to gather signatures is barred political activity.

On candidate nights, even a meet-the-candidates format carries risk if it is not moderated clearly and fairly. Equal time, the same questions, and consistent rules applied to all candidates are the minimum standard.

The absence of a complaint so far is not a legal shield. Commissioner's Decision No. 18,342 (Sherrill-Kenwood Free Library, 2023) illustrates why. In that case, poll workers handed voters a sticker reading "Your Vote Counts" and "Love Your Library" alongside their ballots during a budget vote. The election was challenged. The Commissioner dismissed the appeal because the petitioner could not prove the stickers changed the outcome. The library still had to hire counsel, prepare a formal defense, and respond to a full Commissioner's proceeding. A multi-year practice of posting candidate profiles is a more systematic pattern than a single sticker at a single polling place.

A compliant election notice includes the election date, location, number of open seats, and the names of candidates as they appear on the ballot. Neutral does not mean boring. Emphasize the importance of trustee service, the role of the library, and the community needs in the library's long-range plan. Tell people their vote matters.

Individual trustees and employees retain the right to endorse a candidate personally, so long as it is clear they are not speaking for the library.

Every school district public library conducting its own elections should have a written election procedure reviewed by its own counsel. If your library does not have one, now is the time to develop it. Reach out to Ron with questions.

## New York State Budget: Immigration Protections for Libraries

The final New York State Budget includes a comprehensive set of immigration protections that affect libraries directly. Among the provisions, the Budget will prohibit ICE from entering sensitive locations, including libraries, without a judicial warrant. It will also prohibit local law enforcement from being deputized by ICE for federal civil immigration enforcement, establish a state right to sue federal, state, and local officials for constitutional violations, and strictly prohibit the use of state, local, and school civil resources for civil immigration enforcement activities.

The full set of provisions is summarized by the [Office of Children and Family Services](https://ocfs.ny.gov/main/news/article.php?idx=2759).

No immediate action is required of libraries. Directors should be aware of these protections and may find them useful in conversations with staff and trustees about library policy and patron safety.

## What a School District Audit Tells Us About Library Data Security

In April 2026, the Office of the State Comptroller released an audit of [New York City Public Schools, Privacy and Security of Student Data](https://www.osc.ny.gov/state-agencies/audits/2026/04/23/new-york-city-public-schools-privacy-and-security-student-data-2023-n-6). The audit examined whether NYCPS consistently followed laws and regulations governing the privacy and security of student data. The findings are worth reading carefully, because libraries carry similar obligations to protect patron personally identifiable information, and the gaps the audit identified are the same categories of risk that the Systems Access and Confidentiality of Library Records Policy, the Data Security Policy, and the required Email and Systems Access trainings are designed to address.

The OSC's seven recommendations from the audit translate directly to the library context.

1. **Identify gaps in your data security posture against an established framework.** OWWL's policies draw on NIST guidelines and ALA principles to define what adequate security looks like for library systems.
2. **Develop a mechanism to ensure breach notifications go out within required time frames.** Libraries have obligations under New York's SHIELD Act and CPLR 4509. The Systems Access Policy and Data Security Policy establish procedures for responding to suspected compromises.
3. **Adopt a written data classification policy.** OWWL's policy defines what constitutes patron PII, establishes minimal disclosure as the operating standard, and limits the use of patron data to authorized library purposes.
4. **Maintain an accurate inventory of all systems that access or store protected data.** The Systems Access Policy requires that all Authorized Users be tracked, that account access be reviewed upon separations from service, and that shared accounts be managed carefully.
5. **Ensure that everyone with access to protected data completes required training.** The resolution the Board passes tomorrow evening directly addresses this, requiring all current account holders to complete both the Systems Access and Email Security trainings by September 30, 2026.
6. **Strengthen technical controls over information systems.** OWWL's device requirements, password standards, and restrictions on personal device use for patron data are the technical layer of this work.
7. **Cooperate fully and promptly with oversight inquiries.** The NYCPS audit was complicated in part by delays and incomplete disclosure during the audit process itself. Libraries should maintain documentation practices that support accountability.

The NYCPS audit found that nearly half of data breaches were reported to the State Education Department late, that the district lacked a written data classification policy, and that more than one in four employees did not complete required training in 2024. None of those findings are unique to a school district. They describe gaps that exist in any institution that handles sensitive data without consistent policies and training requirements in place.

The work OWWL and its member libraries have been doing, including the Systems Access Policy, the required trainings, and the Board action tomorrow, is a direct response to exactly these categories of risk.
