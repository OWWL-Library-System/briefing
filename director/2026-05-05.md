## Out-of-System Interlibrary Loan Reminder

If a patron is looking for a title not owned by any OWWL library, OWWL staff can coordinate borrowing from outside the system. Full information and the request form are available on [OWWL Docs](https://docs.owwl.org/Members/InterlibraryLoanRequest).

A few eligibility reminders:

- Items must be at least three years past their publication date (2023 or earlier)
- Patrons are limited to five requests at a time
- Owning libraries set their own due dates. Not all items are renewable. Check the band wrapped around the item for specific circulation information.
- Contact OWWL HQ ILL staff for all renewal requests. Renewals must be approved by the owning library before any due date change is granted to the patron.

The following types of materials are not available through out-of-system ILL:

- Video
- Audio
- Computer software
- Genealogy materials
- Reference materials
- Entire issues of magazines
- Restricted access materials (local history, rare books, closed stacks, and similar)

OWWL staff will communicate the status of all ILL requests, whether approved or denied, along with due dates and any special circumstances such as in-house use only.

Even if a title appears in WorldCat, that does not guarantee OWWL staff can obtain it. Not every library loans materials to other systems.

Questions? Email [ill@owwl.org](mailto:ill@owwl.org).

## The OSC is Auditing Libraries Again: Howard Public Library

The Office of the State Comptroller recently released an audit of [Howard Public Library](https://www.osc.ny.gov/local-government/audits/library/2026/04/24/howard-public-library-board-oversight-2026m-3) focused on board oversight. This is one of the first library audits OSC has released in several years, and it is worth taking seriously.

OSC audits tend to move in waves. When the Comptroller's office begins auditing one type of organization, others in the same category often follow. The release of a library audit is a signal that library governance is on OSC's radar.

The audit examined whether the Howard Public Library board provided adequate oversight of the library's financial operations. While the specific findings apply to Howard Public Library, the issues OSC looks for in board oversight audits are consistent across public institutions. They include whether the board reviews and approves financial transactions, whether policies are in place and followed, and whether the board exercises genuine oversight rather than routine approval.

### What This Means for Your Library

Now is a good time to take an honest look at how your board exercises oversight. A few questions worth discussing at your next board meeting:

- Does the board review and approve all financial claims before payment?
- Are your financial policies current, and does the board understand what they require?
- Does the board receive financial reports with enough detail to ask informed questions?
- Are board minutes accurately reflecting the actions the board takes?

These are not extraordinary standards. They are the baseline OSC expects of any public institution. Libraries that maintain strong governance practices are well positioned if an audit comes their way.

## This Week in OSC Audits

Aside from the Howard Public Library audit, here is a summary of other recent Office of the State Comptroller audits. These audits examine towns, villages, and other local governments across New York. The settings vary, but the themes are consistent ones for any public institution, including libraries.

For directors and trustees, these audits are useful reminders about where governance systems tend to break down and what practices help maintain public trust.

### [Town of Alexander: Town Clerk/Tax Collector](https://www.osc.ny.gov/local-government/audits/town/2026/04/17/town-alexander-town-clerktax-collector-2025m-72)

**Issues identified**

- Collections were not always recorded, deposited, or remitted accurately and on time.
- The board did not conduct the annual audit of the clerk's records required by Town Law.
- Accountability analyses were not completed, leaving errors undetected.

**Takeaways for libraries**

- Treasurers and fiscal officers should maintain consistent records of all collections and disbursements, with deposits made promptly.
- The board has a legal responsibility to audit the treasurer's records annually. This is not optional, and it should not be delegated informally.
- Monthly bank reconciliations give the board an early opportunity to catch discrepancies before they become larger problems.

### [Town of Davenport: Transparency of Fiscal Activities](https://www.osc.ny.gov/local-government/audits/statewide-audit/2026/04/17/town-davenport-transparency-fiscal-activities-s9-25-28)

**Issues identified**

- Financial information was not made sufficiently available to the public.
- Budget and financial documents were not posted or distributed as required.

**Takeaways for libraries**

- Libraries have fiscal transparency obligations. Budget documents, audit reports, and annual financial information should be accessible to the public.
- Posting financial documents on the library's website is a straightforward way to meet this obligation and demonstrate good governance.

### [Town of Sodus: Transparency of Fiscal Activities](https://www.osc.ny.gov/local-government/audits/statewide-audit/2026/04/17/town-sodus-transparency-fiscal-activities-s9-25-38)

**Issues identified**

- Financial activity was not reported to the public in a clear or consistent manner.
- The public had limited ability to understand or scrutinize financial decisions.

**Takeaways for libraries**

- Transparency is not only a legal requirement. It is a governance value. How the library presents financial information to the public reflects how seriously the board takes its public trust responsibilities.
- Clear, accessible financial reporting supports community confidence in the library.

### [Town of Knox: Transparency of Fiscal Activities](https://www.osc.ny.gov/local-government/audits/statewide-audit/2026/04/24/town-knox-transparency-fiscal-activities-s9-25-30)

**Issues identified**

- Fiscal activities were not reported with adequate transparency.
- Opportunities to inform the public about financial decisions were missed.

**Takeaways for libraries**

- Recurring audit findings on transparency across multiple towns suggest this is a systemic weakness in many public institutions, not an outlier issue.
- Libraries that proactively communicate financial information are better positioned when questions arise from the public or from external reviewers.

> Across all of these audits, a consistent pattern appears. Problems rarely begin with fraud or misconduct. They begin with small gaps in oversight, unclear documentation, or policies that drift out of alignment with actual practice. Regular review of financial procedures, procurement policies, and board reporting practices helps prevent those small gaps from becoming larger governance failures.
