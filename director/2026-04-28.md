## NYS Minimum Standards Updates

The Board of Regents has approved an amendment to the Regulations of the Commissioner of Education updating Minimum Standard #4 for public and association libraries. Libraries are now required to maintain a defined set of written policies and review each of them at least once every five years, or earlier if required by law.

The policies now required by the standard are the selection of library materials and reconsideration of such selection; public usage of library space and meeting rooms; codes of conduct; confidentiality of library records; personnel policies that ensure consistent staff management and fair employment practices; disaster preparedness; and financial control policies that fulfill the legal and fiduciary responsibilities of the governing body and promote fiscal oversight, accountability, and sustainable management.

If your library has these policies in place, the five-year review clock is running. If any are missing, developing them is now required, not optional.

I helped the State update the [Helpful Information for Meeting Minimum Public Library Standards](https://nyslibrary.libguides.com/publiclibrarystandards/policies) page with resources for each policy area. I am also updating the [Sample Policy Library on OWWLDocs](https://docs.owwl.org/Community/PolicyLibrary) to include all of these policies. Your library will need to have them board-approved no later than January 1, 2027.

## Email Security Update from Kelsy

Due to a recent security concern, we have disabled a Zimbra setting that automatically added calendar invitations to your OWWL Mail calendar as pending appointments.

A staff member recently received two phishing emails disguised as Microsoft Teams meeting invitations. Both were automatically added to their calendar before anyone could catch them. An example of what these look like is below.

![Phishing invite example]({{ '/assets/images/uploads/2026-02-26calendarinvitephishing02.jpg' | relative_url }})

> **Do not accept or decline calendar invitations that appear suspicious or that you did not expect.** Responding, even to decline, can confirm to the sender that your account is active and make it a target for future attempts.

Calendar invitations you receive will no longer appear automatically in your calendar as pending appointments. Legitimate invitations you do want to accept can still be added to your calendar using the accept link in the invitation email. Accepting a legitimate invitation from the email still adds it to your calendar normally.

If an invitation looks suspicious, mark the email as spam and leave it alone.

Questions? Email [support@owwl.org](mailto:support@owwl.org).

## Plan of Service 2027–2031: Mini Survey #10 on Interlibrary Loans, Holds, and Delivery

As we continue shaping the 2027–2031 Plan of Service, this week's mini survey focuses on interlibrary loans, holds, and delivery.

A clarification on terms that sometimes get used interchangeably. Holds in this survey refers to materials circulated within the OWWL Library System, items requested from other OWWL member libraries. Interlibrary loans refers to materials borrowed from outside the OWWL Library System entirely.

Both are core services, and both depend on systems and infrastructure that the System manages. This survey asks directors to reflect on how these services are working for patrons in their communities, where friction points exist, and where system-level improvements could make the most difference.

## This Week in OSC Audits

Below is a summary of recent Office of the State Comptroller audits. These audits examine towns, villages, school districts, and charter schools across New York. The settings vary, but the themes are consistent ones for any public institution, including libraries. For directors and trustees, these audits are useful reminders about where governance systems tend to break down and what practices help maintain public trust.

Six audits this week. Two are about IT security. Two are about fiscal transparency failures. One is a school district in serious financial trouble. And two are clean audits, which is worth noting, because clean audits happen when governance is working.

[A town and a charter school](https://links-2.govdelivery.com/CL0/https:%2F%2Fwww.osc.ny.gov%2Flocal-government%2Faudits%2Ftown%2F2026%2F04%2F10%2Ftown-plattekill-information-technology-2025m-132%3Futm_medium=email%26utm_source=govDelivery/1/0101019dc44f7e50-6c437bbb-2138-4f75-befb-baba7125d23f-000000/nCBUApeOSIaNHVHSfnpvgBf1rhXqPelyd6NBhox8jSE=452) each received IT audits with overlapping findings. Former employee accounts had not been disabled, breach notification policies had not been adopted, IT contingency plans were not in place, and security awareness training was not provided to staff. These are the same categories the System addresses through our IT policies and the guardrails we put in place for member libraries. If your library follows System policies and takes IT security seriously, you should be in good shape. If you have been putting off routine IT housekeeping, these audits are a useful reminder of what that deferred work actually costs.

[A town](https://links-2.govdelivery.com/CL0/https:%2F%2Fwww.osc.ny.gov%2Flocal-government%2Faudits%2Fstatewide-audit%2F2026%2F04%2F10%2Ftown-clermont-transparency-fiscal-activities-s9-25-27%3Futm_medium=email%26utm_source=govDelivery/1/0101019dc44f7e50-6c437bbb-2138-4f75-befb-baba7125d23f-000000/Z2RIowKpt50Y0hh_ZF_oR2UwKrOlNYJu6-HzEhJerKw=452) failed to file required annual financial reports and did not provide its board with complete monthly financial information. Without reliable reports, the board could not monitor the town's financial position or make informed decisions. Residents, taxpayers, and state reviewers were left without access to current fiscal information.

[A school district](https://links-2.govdelivery.com/CL0/https:%2F%2Fwww.osc.ny.gov%2Flocal-government%2Faudits%2Fschool-district%2F2026%2F04%2F10%2Fsouth-country-central-school-district-enhanced-budget-review-b26-7-4%3Futm_medium=email%26utm_source=govDelivery/1/0101019dc44f7e50-6c437bbb-2138-4f75-befb-baba7125d23f-000000/BE1XXYSxATWGYlHrivhXFv-Gl5qpYijzPt--xVY74gs=452) is on track to end the current fiscal year with a deficit of approximately $8.7 million, with no surplus fund balance to absorb it. Inaccurate budget estimates contributed to the shortfall even after spending freezes and personnel layoffs were implemented. The district will likely need to borrow $6 million to balance its next budget. Borrowing to fund operating expenses deepens the problem rather than solving it.

[A village](https://links-2.govdelivery.com/CL0/https:%2F%2Fwww.osc.ny.gov%2Flocal-government%2Faudits%2Fvillage%2F2026%2F04%2F10%2Fvillage-suffern-budget-review-b26-6-3%3Futm_medium=email%26utm_source=govDelivery/1/0101019dc44f7e50-6c437bbb-2138-4f75-befb-baba7125d23f-000000/ZcwyHfS5dyN09ID-AM16yrhVI6xIyNnSEdHRHPfHVmE=452) received a routine budget review with no significant findings. The tax levy is within legal limits and revenue and expenditure projections appear reasonable. This is what a clean audit looks like. It does not make headlines, but it reflects consistent attention to the basics.

[A school district](https://links-2.govdelivery.com/CL0/https:%2F%2Fwww.osc.ny.gov%2Flocal-government%2Faudits%2Fschool-district%2F2026%2F04%2F10%2Fwyandanch-union-free-school-district-budget-review-b26-7-2%3Futm_medium=email%26utm_source=govDelivery/1/0101019dc44f7e50-6c437bbb-2138-4f75-befb-baba7125d23f-000000/gCWVi3PJgpYijzPt--xVY74gs=452) also received a clean budget review. **10 lessons from this week's audits**

1. Disable departing staff accounts the same week someone leaves. An active account for a former employee is an unlocked door.
2. Adopt a breach notification policy. New York State Technology Law requires one. If your library does not have one, ask the System for help.
3. Have an IT contingency plan. It does not need to be long. It needs to answer what happens when things go wrong and who is responsible for fixing them.
4. Provide IT security awareness training to all staff, every year. Most breaches start with a person, not a technical failure.
5. Put your IT service agreements in writing. A handshake arrangement with a vendor offers no protection when something goes wrong.
6. Give your board real financial reports every month. Budget-to-actual comparisons, cash balances, fund balance estimates. Trustees cannot ask good questions about information they have never seen.
7. File your annual financial report on time. It is a legal requirement, and it is how the public and state reviewers understand whether the institution is being managed responsibly.
8. Build your budget on trend analysis, not optimism. Inaccurate estimates do not stay hidden. They surface as deficits.
9. Maintain a fund balance. It is not money sitting idle. It is the cushion that absorbs the unexpected without forcing layoffs or borrowing.
10. Do the basics consistently. Two of this week's audits were clean. Those institutions are not doing anything extraordinary. They are doing ordinary things reliably.

> Across all of these audits, a consistent pattern appears. Problems rarely begin with fraud or misconduct. They begin with small gaps in oversight, unclear documentation, or policies that drift out of alignment with actual practice. Regular review of financial procedures, procurement policies, and board reporting practices helps prevent those small gaps from becoming larger governance failures.
