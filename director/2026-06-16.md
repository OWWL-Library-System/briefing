## Security Alert: Fraudulent Sites Impersonating OWWL

We want to make you aware of a recurring security issue involving websites impersonating OWWL. Two fraudulent sites (owwi.org and owwll.org) were registered with names designed to closely mimic our own (owwl.org). This tactic is used by cybercriminals to send emails that appear to come from legitimate OWWL and member library staff. The goal is for the fake site to look so close to the real thing that someone may not notice the discrepancy and engage with the cybercriminal on the other end.

The first time we were notified of this was in February:

<img src="https://docs.owwl.org/pub/Members/DirectorBriefing/2026-02-23%20owwi.org%20phishing.jpg?_t=1781547916" alt="Screenshot of phishing email from owwi.org in February 2026" style="max-width:100%;height:auto;display:block;">

The second email, which happened last week, strangely did not contain any links, but listed Ron's responsibilities and closed with a member library name and phone number:

<img src="https://docs.owwl.org/pub/Members/DirectorBriefing/2026-06-04%20owwll.org%20phishing.jpg?_t=1781547916" alt="Screenshot of phishing email from owwll.org in June 2026" style="max-width:100%;height:auto;display:block;">

Both of these imposter websites have been reported and taken down. However, there is always the possibility that similar sites could be registered in the future, so we wanted to send out an alert.

**What to watch for:**

- Check the sender's email address carefully, even if the sender's name looks legitimate. Look for subtle misspellings such as an extra letter (owwll), a swapped character (owwi), or a missing letter in the domain.
- Be suspicious of any email that creates urgency, asks you to click a link, open an attachment, or provide credentials — even if it appears to come from a director, colleague, Board member, or System staff. Fraudulent emails may also feel "off" with an abrupt request, a vague question (i.e., "Are you available?" or "Can you do something for me?"), or a style that is vastly different from how you know that person to normally communicate.
- Hover over links before clicking to verify the destination URL matches a trusted domain. For example, in the first screenshot above, the link says "Review Your Email Address" but hovering reveals the true destination is "owwi.org" — a fraudulent site. (Also, we never require verification or review of your email address.)
- When in doubt, verify the email's legitimacy through a separate channel (phone call or in-person) before taking action.

Staying alert is one of the most effective defenses against phishing attacks. If you receive a suspicious email, do not reply, click any links, or open any attachments. Please forward it to support so we can investigate.

## Security Advisory: Staff Contact Information on Library Websites

Security considerations for staff contact information on library websites

Many libraries publish staff directories on their websites that include individual names, job titles, direct email addresses, and phone numbers. This is a well-intentioned practice. It helps patrons connect quickly with the right person and signals a welcoming and transparent organization.

However, this information also creates a meaningful security risk that we want to bring to your attention: spear phishing.

**What is spear phishing?**

Unlike generic phishing emails sent to thousands of random addresses, spear phishing is a targeted attack. Cybercriminals research a specific organization before crafting their message, using real names, titles, and relationships to make fraudulent emails appear credible.

A publicly listed staff directory gives cybercriminals everything they need to:

- Address an employee by name and reference their role;
- Impersonate a colleague, director, Board Trustee, or vendor the employee would recognize;
- Craft urgent, plausible requests (wire transfers, gift card purchases, credential changes, direct deposit changes, etc.);
- Target staff most likely to have access to finances or sensitive patron data.

For example, an attacker could see that your library has a Finance Clerk (or Bookkeeper) and a Director listed on your website. They could then send your Finance Clerk an email appearing to come from the Director — using the Director's real name and title — requesting an urgent financial transaction. This type of attack is increasingly common and effective.

**Our recommendation**

We recommend that libraries replace individual staff contact listings with a single general contact email address for the library (e.g., yourlibrary@owwl.org).

Best practices for this approach:

- Designate a staff member to monitor the shared inbox who is experienced at recognizing suspicious messages. This person should know what phishing attempts look like and have a process for flagging and discarding them.
- Route inquiries appropriately from behind the scenes. Patrons still get a response, they just contact the library as a whole rather than a named individual. Internally, staff can forward or handle messages as needed without exposing individual contact details publicly.
- Retain individual contact info for those who need it, shared privately and on a need-to-know basis rather than published on the public website.
- Consider a contact form as an alternative or supplement. Contact forms reduce direct email harvesting by bots while still giving patrons an easy way to reach out.

**Balancing service and security**

We recognize this represents a tradeoff. Publishing staff names and direct contact information is genuinely good customer service, and some patrons and community partners prefer knowing exactly who they're reaching. We are not suggesting that libraries become less accessible, only that the public website may not be the right place to expose that level of detail.

The goal is to keep your staff and the library safer without reducing the quality of patron service. A well-monitored shared inbox, responsive to all inquiries, achieves both.

**Questions?**

If you have questions about this guidance, would like help evaluating your current website, or want to discuss how to structure a shared inbox workflow, please open a support ticket.

This memo is part of OWWL's ongoing effort to support member libraries with cybersecurity awareness and best practices.

## Help with Website Domains

We are exploring a new optional service that would allow member libraries to have their website domain names (i.e., anywherepubliclibrary.org) centrally managed and renewed by the OWWL Library System. This email provides an overview of the proposed service and invites interested directors to register their interest.

**About the service**

Under this service, OWWL would manage domain registration and annual renewals on behalf of participating libraries, then invoice the library for the cost. The goal is to reduce administrative burden for libraries that lack dedicated IT/tech staff and to prevent service disruptions caused by missed renewals.

Key features of the proposed service include:

- Optional enrollment: Any library that would like to continue managing their own domain can do so.
- Domain ownership remains with the library: Even though OWWL will pay domain costs upfront on the library's behalf and invoice for reimbursement, the domain belongs to the library. You retain full rights to transfer or move your domain at any time.
- Cost passthrough: OWWL will pay domain registration, renewal, and privacy protection (see below) upfront, then invoice the library for the full amount. Libraries will be invoiced at cost — there is no markup or administrative fee.
- Domain privacy protection: OWWL will enroll participating libraries in domain privacy/protection. This hides your registration details from public lookups, which reduces exposure to scam invoices sent to your email or mailing address. Depending on the registrar, domain privacy protection is included at minimal ($2-$15/year) or no additional cost. Any added cost will be reflected in your invoice.
- Centralized oversight: OWWL will handle registrar communication, renewal reminders, and DNS coordination on your behalf.
- Continuity through staff transitions: A recurring challenge for libraries has been previous directors registering domains under email addresses that are closed upon separation of service, or using a personal mobile number for two-factor authentication (2FA) login codes. This can lock future directors out of the domain registrar account and lead to service disruptions, and, in some cases, loss of the library's domain entirely. Under this service, OWWL's Computer and Network Services (CANS) department will retain account login information, and 2FA codes will go to a mobile number owned by OWWL, ensuring access is never tied to a single staff member.

**Current status**

We are not yet ready to begin onboarding libraries into this service. Before launching, we are working through the following:

- Evaluating and selecting a domain registrar to work with;
- Drafting service policies, including procedures for domain transfers and offboarding;
- Developing a formal service agreement for participating libraries;
- Assessing interest among member libraries.

We will communicate a launch timeline once these items are in place. Libraries that express interest now will receive details when the service is ready.

**How to express interest**

If you are interested in participating in this service when it becomes available, please open a support ticket with the subject line "Domain Management Interest." We will add you to a contact list and reach out with next steps when the service is ready to launch.

Expressing interest is not a commitment; you will have the opportunity to review the service agreement before enrolling.

**Questions?**

If you have questions or feedback about this proposed service, please reach out via a support ticket. We welcome input from directors as we finalize the service design.

## This Week in OSC Audits

Below is a summary of recent Office of the State Comptroller audits. These audits examine towns and villages across New York. The settings vary, but the themes are consistent ones for any public institution, including libraries.

For directors and trustees, these audits are useful reminders about where governance systems tend to break down and what practices help maintain public trust.

### [A Town: Board Oversight — Code Enforcement Revenues](https://www.osc.ny.gov/local-government/audits/town/2026/05/29/town-middlesex-board-oversight-code-enforcement-revenues-2026m-4)

A town was audited for its oversight of code enforcement revenues over a ten-year period. Auditors found that town officials failed to ensure revenues were accurately billed, collected, deposited, recorded, or reported. More than $31,000 in engineering fees went unrecovered. The board did not receive regular written reports from the code enforcement office, and financial duties were not adequately separated. The former code enforcement officer's state certification was also found to have lapsed during 2023, 2024, and part of 2025 while she remained employed.

**Takeaways for libraries**

- When a department or function has no written policies governing billing, collections, or reporting, errors accumulate quietly. Libraries should have documented procedures for every revenue stream, no matter how small.
- Financial duties should be separated so that no single staff member controls billing, collection, and recording without any independent check.
- The board should receive regular written reports from any staff member handling money or managing a function with financial implications. If those reports are not coming, that is the time to ask why.

### [A Town: Transparency of Fiscal Activities](https://www.osc.ny.gov/local-government/audits/statewide-audit/2026/05/29/town-butternuts-transparency-fiscal-activities-s9-25-24)

A town was audited for its fiscal transparency. Auditors found that the board did not conduct an annual audit of the supervisor's financial records as required by Town Law, and the supervisor did not file annual financial reports with the State Comptroller from 2020 through 2024 as required by state law. The supervisor also did not provide the board with complete, accurate, or reliable monthly financial reports.

**Takeaways for libraries**

- Filing required financial reports is a statutory obligation, not an administrative courtesy. Library boards should confirm annually that all required state filings are current.
- A board that does not receive complete monthly financial reports cannot monitor the library's fiscal position or make informed decisions. Directors should present reports that give trustees enough information to ask meaningful questions.
- The board's annual review of the treasurer's records is the library equivalent of this audit finding. It should happen every year and be documented in the minutes.

> Across all of these audits, a consistent pattern appears. Problems rarely begin with fraud or misconduct. They begin with small gaps in oversight, unclear documentation, or policies that drift out of alignment with actual practice. Regular review of financial procedures, procurement policies, and board reporting practices helps prevent those small gaps from becoming larger governance failures.
