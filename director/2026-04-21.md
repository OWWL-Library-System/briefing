## Minimum Standards Update — New Policy Requirements Now in Effect

The NYS Board of Regents recently approved an amendment to the Regulations of the Commissioner of Education, updating Minimum Standard #4 for public and association libraries. The change expands the board-approved written policies requirement to include a specific list of policies that must be reviewed and updated at least once every five years, or earlier if required by law.

The standard now requires written policies covering the selection of library materials and reconsideration of such selection; public usage of library space and meeting rooms; codes of conduct; and confidentiality of library records. It also requires internal library policies covering personnel policies that ensure consistent staff management and fair employment practices; disaster preparedness; and financial control policies that fulfill the legal and fiduciary responsibilities of the governing body and promote fiscal oversight, accountability, and sustainable management.

If your library already has these policies in place, the five-year review clock is now running. If any are missing, developing them is no longer optional.

To support libraries in this work, PULISDO and the New York State Library have updated the [Helpful Information for Meeting Minimum Public Library Standards](https://nyslibrary.libguides.com/publiclibrarystandards/policies) webpage with resources for each policy area.

A webinar series begins next week with an overview session, followed by sessions focused on each new element with real-world examples from libraries.

## FY 2027 State Aid for Library Construction — Intent to Apply

Libraries considering a construction project for FY 2027 State Aid funding need to start here. The Intent to Apply is the first required step in the Construction Aid process, and the deadline is **May 31, 2026**. Late or incomplete submissions may result in ineligibility for this funding cycle.

The Intent to Apply allows OWWL and the Construction Aid Award Committee to confirm basic project eligibility, assess readiness and risk, and develop preliminary funding recommendations. Submitting does not guarantee funding. Libraries may not begin the full application until their Intent to Apply has been reviewed and approved at the recommended funding level.

**What you will need to complete the form**

- A brief description of the proposed project
- An approximate project timeline
- Estimated total project cost
- Contractor quotes showing total project cost (architect estimates are not acceptable at this stage)
- Confirmation that all non-award funds are secured and available

**Key program requirements**

- Only costs incurred on or after July 1, 2026 may be paid with Construction Aid funds.
- Projects must not be completed before the System application deadline, typically late August or early September.
- General maintenance and routine repairs are not eligible.
- All contractor quotes must be dated within Fiscal Year 2026.
- Libraries must demonstrate that their full project cost is covered, including the local share and any costs beyond the potential award. Funds must be formally committed and available, not anticipated or contingent.

**Projects must address at least one New York State priority**

- Increased effectiveness of library service through improved space or capacity
- More efficient use of the building, including energy conservation or staff efficiency
- Improved access to library services for all users, including people with disabilities
- Provision of library services to geographically isolated or economically disadvantaged communities

The OWWL Board of Trustees approves all local applications. Questions are welcome early in the process. Contact Ron or Kelly before you submit. Additional program information is available through the [New York State Library, Division of Library Development](https://www.nysl.nysed.gov/libdev/construc).

## When Can a Trustee Term be "Held Over"?

The Western New York Library Resources Council published a detailed answer to this question on April 17, and the short version is: it depends on your library type.

For association libraries, the Not-For-Profit Corporation Law allows trustees to hold over until a successor is elected or appointed, and bylaws can reflect that explicitly. For public libraries, the Public Officers Law goes further and actually requires it — a trustee whose term has expired continues to serve until a successor is in place, unless the position is abolished or the trustee resigns. For cooperative library systems, including OWWL, the same general holdover principle applies, with one important limit. A holdover cannot extend a trustee's service past the consecutive term limit set by Education Law Section 255.

The article includes suggested bylaw language for each library type, which is worth reviewing if your bylaws are silent on this question.

[Read more at wnylrc.org](https://wnylrc.org/raq/when-can-trustee-term-be-held-over)

## Library Card Registration — A Project in Progress

At the March 2026 OWWLDAC meeting, directors raised questions about library card registration practices and the records retention obligations that come with them. Those questions pointed to a real and shared challenge. Under LGS-1, libraries that issue their own cards and collect registration information are responsible for retaining those records on a defined schedule. For many member libraries, meeting that obligation consistently is a significant administrative burden.

One possible path forward is a shared approach to card registration — one where OWWL establishes a common registration framework that member libraries can use, easing local retention obligations while maintaining the protections patrons expect. This is early work. Nothing has been decided, and OWWLDAC will be part of shaping whatever approach makes sense.

If this is something your library has navigated or thought about, your experience would be valuable to the conversation. Reach out to Ron if you want to share it before the next OWWLDAC discussion.

## Plan of Service 2027–2031 — Mini Survey #9 on Coordinated Outreach

As we continue shaping the 2027–2031 Plan of Service, this week's mini survey focuses on Coordinated Outreach.

Under New York State Education Law §273 and Commissioner's Regulation §90.3, public library systems are required to provide Coordinated Outreach Library Services directly and through their member libraries. The purpose of this program is to reach those who are most in need and who are often not regular library users. The populations the program is designed to serve include people who are educationally disadvantaged, members of ethnic or minority groups in need of special library services, those who are unemployed, residents of areas underserved by a library, and people who are blind, physically disabled, have developmental or learning disabilities, or are aged or confined to an institution.

OWWL's Coordinated Outreach program is a system responsibility, but member libraries are part of how that work gets done. This survey asks directors to reflect on how outreach to underserved populations currently works in their communities, where the gaps are, and where system-level support or coordination could make the most difference.

## System Direct Access Plan 2027–2031 — Open for Comment

**Deadline: April 30, 2026**

OWWL is seeking feedback from member library directors and boards on the draft Direct Access Plan for 2027–2031 before it moves forward for formal approval.

**What is the Direct Access Plan?** It is a required component of the OWWL System's Plan of Service. It governs how library services are made available to all residents within the System's boundaries, including those living outside any member library's chartered service area. It also outlines the conditions under which member libraries may request modifications to unrestricted direct access.

**What is new in this draft.** This draft includes several updates from the current 2022–2026 plan.

- A new negotiation procedure that requires the System to work with municipalities on funding solutions before any restrictions can be imposed
- Updated language affirming the System's role in supporting equitable access
- A new commitment to produce a report identifying unserved areas across Ontario, Wayne, Wyoming, and Livingston Counties

Directors and boards are asked to review the draft and share feedback.

> **Note:** No board action is needed at this time. Once feedback has been reviewed and incorporated, the OWWL Board of Trustees will approve the plan. Following that, a majority of member library boards will need to approve the plan before submission to the Division of Library Development. Separate instructions will follow when that step is ready.

Questions? Contact Ron at [rkirsop@owwl.org](mailto:rkirsop@owwl.org).

## This Week in OSC Audits

Below is a summary of recent Office of the State Comptroller audits. These audits examine cities, villages, and school districts across New York. The settings vary, but the themes are consistent ones for any public institution, including libraries. For directors and trustees, these audits are useful reminders about where governance systems tend to break down and what practices help maintain public trust.

Eight audits this week. The theme is consistent. People in charge of money did not keep track of the money, and the boards responsible for oversight were not getting the information they needed to notice.

[A city](https://www.osc.ny.gov/local-government/audits/city/2026/04/03/city-mechanicville-separation-payments-2025m-59) received three separate audits covering separation payments, procurement, and financial oversight. Separation payments were miscalculated with no written procedures in place to catch the errors. Of a $2.26 million sample of purchases, $760,000 did not follow procurement policy or statutory requirements, and in some cases officials signed off on their own purchases. Bank reconciliations had large unresolved variances, and two years of required annual financial reports were not filed on time. The governing body could not assess the city's financial condition because it was not receiving reliable information.

[A village](https://www.osc.ny.gov/local-government/audits/village/2026/04/03/village-sherburne-budgeting-2025m-83) adopted budgets that underestimated revenues and overestimated appropriations for years running. Taxpayers were assessed more than was needed, unplanned surpluses totaling roughly $1.1 million accumulated, and the budgets did not comply with state law or OSC guidance. The board was planning for deficits that never materialized, which means it was not working from an accurate picture of the village's finances.

[A second village](https://www.osc.ny.gov/local-government/audits/village/2026/04/03/village-victory-records-and-reports-2025m-94) filed its annual financial reports as many as 1,322 days late. Accounting records were inaccurate, bank reconciliations were not properly prepared, and monthly financial reports to the board lacked basic information. Over three years late is not a filing delay. It is an absence of accountability.

[A third village](https://www.osc.ny.gov/local-government/audits/village/2026/04/03/village-youngstown-employee-benefits-and-payroll-2025m-123) did not maintain accurate leave records or ensure payroll payments were properly approved. Auditors found $9,802 in unsupported payments, 1,524 unapproved leave hours, and 313 compensatory hours accrued without authorization. Payroll is often the largest line item in a budget. Board approval of payroll does not mean much if the underlying records are not reliable.

[A fourth village](https://www.osc.ny.gov/local-government/audits/village/2026/04/03/village-churchville-procurement-2025m-140) did not seek competition for purchases totaling $664,748 out of approximately $1.4 million reviewed. In two cases totaling $31,397, no written quotes were obtained at all. Officials approved their own purchases, and the procurement policy did not specify when board approval was required. A policy that does not define thresholds or approval requirements is not much of a policy.

[A school district](https://www.osc.ny.gov/local-government/audits/school-district/2026/04/03/south-orangetown-central-school-district-audit-follow-2022m-24-f) was revisited on a 2022 audit finding that network user accounts for former employees, contractors, and generic users had not been disabled. Four recommendations were issued. By September 2025, only one had been fully implemented. Three remained partially addressed. A recommendation that sits unfinished for three years was not taken seriously.

None of this starts with bad intentions. It starts with no one assigned to check, no one following up, and policies that exist on paper but not in practice.

**So your library does not end up in one of these**

The same problems show up in audit after audit. Here is what actually prevents them.

Assign someone to reconcile accounts every month, and make sure a second person reviews it. Not because you do not trust the first person, but because errors are easier to catch when two sets of eyes are on the numbers.

File your annual financial report on time. It is a legal requirement, and falling behind creates a backlog that is much harder to dig out of than it is to avoid.

Give your board real financial reports every month. Budget-to-actual comparisons, cash balances, fund balance estimates. Trustees cannot ask good questions about information they have never seen.

Review your vendor bills. Subscriptions, service contracts, devices. Things that get set up and forgotten tend to keep generating charges long after they stop being useful.

When an auditor or reviewer makes a recommendation, assign it to someone with a deadline. The follow-up audit will notice.

> Across all of these audits, a consistent pattern appears. Problems rarely begin with fraud or misconduct. They begin with small gaps in oversight, unclear documentation, or policies that drift out of alignment with actual practice. Regular review of financial procedures, procurement policies, and board reporting practices helps prevent those small gaps from becoming larger governance failures.

## AI as a Tool, Not a Shortcut

A recent article in the Harvard Business Review identified a fault line running through organizations that have committed to AI. Executives tend to see it as transformative and already delivering returns, while the managers responsible for making it work day to day hold a more grounded view. The gap is not about enthusiasm. It is about who has to deal with the results when the tool falls short.

Libraries are not exempt from this dynamic. AI tools are genuinely useful. I use them regularly and find them valuable for drafting, summarizing, organizing, and working through complex questions. The technology has real capabilities, and dismissing it outright would be a mistake.

The risk is not AI itself. The risk is AI content that goes out the door without a knowledgeable person reviewing it first.

When a staff member uses an AI tool to draft a social media post, a policy summary, a program description, or a response to a patron question, the output may be fluent and confident and wrong. It may reflect a policy that does not match your library's actual practice. It may describe a program that does not reflect your community. It may answer a question in a way that contradicts your board's decisions. The tool does not know any of that. The staff member reviewing the output does.

This is not a reason to prohibit AI use. It is a reason to be clear about what responsible use looks like. Any content generated with AI assistance should be reviewed by a staff member with enough knowledge of the library's policies, procedures, and community to catch what the tool cannot know. That review is not a formality. It is the step that makes the content usable.

Libraries that establish that expectation now, before a problem surfaces, are in a better position than those that address it after the fact.
